  \begin{block}{Background}

    Converting unstructured longitudinal medication histories from the EHR into
    structured research data relies on \textbf{manual chart abstraction}, a
    resource-intensive process that constrains retrospective and prospective
    studies alike and limits how readily these data can inform clinical
    decision-making. Increasingly capable large language models (LLMs) may
    reduce this burden, but their reliability against human abstraction remains
    largely unquantified.

    \vspace{0.4em}
    We evaluated a \textbf{proof-of-concept LLM medication-extraction pipeline}
    against human chart abstraction in pediatric immune behavioral health
    (IBH), a setting where patients carry long, frequently revised medication
    histories that make abstraction especially demanding. We report agreement
    and adjudicated accuracy separately.

    \vspace{0.4em}
    \textbf{Key research questions:}
    \vspace{-0.6em}   % pulls up the top of the itemized research questions
    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{Agreement / Alignment:} How often do the two methods
            produce the same result?
      \item \textbf{Accuracy:} Where they differ, which method is correct?
    \end{itemize}
    \vspace{-0.4em}   % pulls up the bottom of the Background block
  \end{block}

  \vspace{-0.6em}     % shrinks the gap before the Methods block
  \begin{block}{Methods}

    \textbf{Design:} \textbf{Single-patient pilot} in one of the clinic's most
    complex medication-collection cases. EHR and REDCap data spanning the
    patient's first visit (April 2024) through their most recent encounter
    (June 2026) were extracted via STARR (Stanford's research extraction tool)
    and manual CSV download, yielding \textbf{280 clinical notes} from a range
    of Stanford clinics, \textbf{39 patient-questionnaire responses}, and
    \textbf{313 Epic medication entries}. These \textbf{same source materials}
    were given to both the AI pipeline and the IBH clinic research assistants
    (RAs) who manually abstracted the medications, ensuring an equal-information
    comparison.

    \vspace{0.6em}
    \textbf{Extraction/Reconciliation:} Each source was extracted
    independently by Claude Opus 4.6 via SecureGPT (Stanford's
    PHI-compliant institutional gateway), then consolidated into a single AI list using a
    confidence-weighted, source-reliability-aware reconciliation.

\vspace{0.6em}
    \textbf{Comparison:} Independent manual abstraction by the same RAs,
    compared against the AI list across three layers:

    \vspace{0.2em}
    \begin{tabularx}{\colwidth}{>{\centering\arraybackslash}X >{\centering\arraybackslash}X >{\centering\arraybackslash}X}

      \bottomrule

    \vspace{0.6em}
    \textbf{Adjudication:} Substantive disagreements were judged by chart review
    as AI-correct, human-correct, both-defensible, or both-wrong, with the cause
    of each classified.

    \vspace{0.6em}
\textbf{Scope:} Across the sources and time frame the AI screened for \textbf{126 distinct
    medications} spanning the clinic's six tracked classes: DMARDs, antibiotics,
    psychiatric medications, IVIG, NSAIDs, and steroids.

\begin{block}{Agreement: Status, Sources, and Confidence}

  \textbf{Status.}
  Medication status (ongoing vs.\ stopped) agreed in \textbf{90\% (26/29)}.

  \vspace{0.6em}
  \textbf{Source reliability by field.}
  Each source's concordance with the human abstraction varied by field, which is
  what the confidence-weighted, source-aware reconciliation exploits:

 \vspace{0.3em}
  \begin{center}
    \begin{tabular}{lcc}
      \toprule
      \textbf{Field} & \textbf{Most reliable source (agreement)} & \textbf{Avg.\ (3 sources)} \\
      \midrule
      Start date             & Epic Meds (\textbf{96\%})            & 84\% \\
      Stop date              & Clinical Notes (\textbf{81\%})       & 67\% \\
      Status                 & Parent Questionnaire (\textbf{96\%}) & 92\% \\
      Discontinuation reason & Clinical Notes (\textbf{64\%})       & 48\% \\
      \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
  \end{center}

  \vspace{0.3em}
  Each field has a clear best source, which the reconciliation weights
  accordingly.

  \vspace{0.7em}
  \textbf{Confidence calibration.}
  The AI's self-reported confidence tracks its agreement with the human, so
  disagreement concentrates in the flagged, low-confidence extractions:

  \vspace{0.2em}
  \begin{center}
    \begin{tabular}{lc}
      \toprule
      \textbf{AI confidence} & \textbf{Concordance with human} \\
      \midrule
      High   & \textbf{92\%} \\
      Medium & 89\% \\
      Low    & \textbf{50\%} \\
      \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
  \end{center}

  \vspace{0.2em}
  Review is therefore easy to target: scrutinize the low-confidence extractions
  and trust the rest.

  \begin{block}{Confidence vs.\ Validated Accuracy}

    Each contest was tagged with the AI episode's reconciliation confidence
    (high / medium / low). \textbf{Merit-loss rate} = (human beats it + both
    wrong) / total, excluding could-not-be-known ties.

    \vspace{0.5em}
    \begin{center}
      \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Confidence} & \textbf{N} & \textbf{AI correct} & \textbf{Human beats it} & \textbf{Merit-loss} \\
        \midrule
        High   & 6 & 2 & 0 & \textbf{0\%}  \\
        Medium & 5 & 2 & 1 & \textbf{20\%} \\
        Low    & 5 & 1 & 1 & \textbf{40\%} \\
        \bottomrule
      \end{tabular}
    \end{center}

    \vspace{0.4em}
    High confidence never lost on the merits (its 3 non-AI-correct outcomes
    were all could-not-be-known). The single genuine reasoning error sat at
    medium, indicating the model under-discriminates at the top. The
    \textbf{low-confidence tag is a reliable verify signal} for targeted human
    review.

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Conclusions}

    \begin{itemize}
      \item LLM extraction was \textbf{highly concordant} with human abstraction
            on the structural backbone: drug existence, episode timing, and
            medication status.
      \item Disagreement concentrated in \textbf{discontinuation reason (60\%)},
            largely attributable to input limitations rather than model error;
            only 1 of 17 disagreements was a genuine AI reasoning error.
      \item \textbf{Low-confidence flags concentrated genuine errors}, supporting
            a workflow in which humans review only low-confidence extractions.
      \item Estimated compute cost was \textbf{\$10--28 vs.\ $\sim$\$400 in RA
            labor}: a more than tenfold reduction.
      \item Findings are \textbf{descriptive (n=1)} and require multi-patient
            validation, but accuracy, cost, and time savings support feasibility
            for scaling with confidence-guided human review.
    \end{itemize}

  \end{block}
