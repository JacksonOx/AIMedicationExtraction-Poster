\begin{column}{\colwide}  

  \begin{block}{Background}
    Converting unstructured longitudinal medication histories from the EHR into
    structured research data relies on \textbf{manual chart abstraction}, a
    resource-intensive and error-prone process \cite{ntinopoulos2025} that
    constrains retrospective and prospective studies alike and limits how
    readily this data can inform clinical decision-making. Increasingly capable
    large language models (LLMs) may reduce this burden \cite{ntinopoulos2025,shao2026},
    but their reliability against human abstraction, especially for medication
    discontinuation, remains largely unquantified \cite{shao2026}.

    We evaluated a \textbf{proof-of-concept LLM medication-extraction pipeline}
    against human chart abstraction in pediatric immune behavioral health
    (IBH), a setting where patients carry long, frequently revised medication
    histories that make abstraction especially demanding. We report agreement
    and adjudicated accuracy separately.

    \textbf{Key research questions:}
    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{Agreement / Alignment:} How often do the two methods
            produce the same result?
      \item \textbf{Accuracy:} Where they differ, which method is correct?
    \end{itemize}
  \end{block}

  \vspace{-0.6em}
  \begin{block}{Methods}

    \textbf{Design:} A \textbf{single-patient pilot} on one of the clinic's most
    complex medication-collection cases. We extracted all Epic and REDCap data from the
    patient's first visit (4/2024) through their most recent encounter
    (6/2026) via STARR (Stanford's research extraction tool)
    and manual CSV download, yielding \textbf{280 clinical notes} from a range
    of Stanford clinics, \textbf{39 patient-questionnaire responses}, and
    \textbf{313 Epic medication entries}. These \textbf{same source materials}
    were available to both the LLM pipeline and the IBH clinic research assistants
    (RAs) who manually abstracted the medications.

    \textbf{Adjudication:} Substantive disagreements were judged by human chart review

    \vspace{0.4em}
    \textbf{Scope:} Between 4/2024 and 6/2026, the LLM screened for
    \textbf{126 distinct medications} across \textbf{six classes}:
    \textit{DMARDs, antibiotics, psychotropics, IVIG, NSAIDs, and steroids}.

  \begin{block}{Agreement: Presence and Status}
  \vspace{-0.2cm}
  \begin{center}
    \includesvg[width=1\colnarrow]{fig_presence_status}
  \end{center}
  \vspace{-1.0cm}
  Of \textbf{21} validated medications encompassing \textbf{32} discrete medication courses, \textbf{19 (90.5\%)} were recorded by

  and captured \textbf{two additional} validated medications. Medication status
  (ongoing vs.\ stopped) agreed in \textbf{90\% (26/29)}.

   \vspace{0.8cm}
    \begin{center}

    Among matched episodes, dates converge quickly: \textbf{93\%} of start and

    fell within thirty days.
  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Agreement: Sources}
  \vspace{0.8em}
\textbf{Source reliability by field:} Each field had a clear best source (named
below it), lifting agreement well above the 3-source average:
    \begin{center}
    \vspace{-0.2cm}
    \includesvg[width=1\colnarrow]{fig_source_reliability}
    \end{center}
    \vspace{0.6cm}
Notably, the strongest source differs by field: structured Epic data wins for
start dates, clinical notes for stop dates and reasons, and parent report for
status. Source choice measurably affects agreement, yet this reconciliation
applied fixed, estimated weights. Empirically derived weights, adapted per
patient with larger samples, could improve future accuracy.
\end{block}

 \begin{block}{Accuracy: Adjudication (17 Contests)}
  \vspace{-0.4em}
  \begin{center}
    \includesvg[width=1\colnarrow]{fig13_adjudication_donut}
  \end{center}
\textbf{Who was correct:} LLM in \textbf{5}, human in \textbf{2}, both defensible
in \textbf{6}, not resolvable from available inputs in \textbf{3}, and both
wrong in \textbf{1}.

\textbf{Why they differed:} most disagreements were not model error: \textbf{6}
were defensible clinical-judgment ties and \textbf{3} could not be determined
from the data given to the LLM (STARR omissions or clinician-only knowledge). Of
the rest, the LLM was correct in \textbf{5} (human error/omission) and the human in \textbf{2}; only \textbf{1} was a
genuine LLM reasoning error.
\vspace{-0.4em}
\end{block}

\begin{block}{Accuracy: Discontinuation Reason}
  \vspace{0.3em}
  \begin{center}
    \includesvg[width=1\colnarrow]{fig12_discontinuation_reason}
  \end{center}
Discontinuation reason was the weakest field for agreement at \textbf{60\%
(9/15)}. But when all \textbf{six} disagreements were adjudicated, only one was
a genuine LLM error: three were defensible framings of the same event, one LLM
answer was judged more accurate than the RA's, and one could not have been
determined by the LLM because the reasoning lived in patient messages it was
never given.
\vspace{-0.6em}
\end{block}

 \begin{block}{Confidence Calibration}
   \vspace{-0.4em}
  \begin{center}
    \includesvg[width=0.95\colnarrow]{fig_confidence_calibration}
  \end{center}

  \vspace{0.2em}
 The LLM's confidence is well calibrated to its accuracy: every adjudicated loss
was low-confidence, and no high- or medium-confidence answer lost on review.
\textbf{Thus, low confidence is a reliable signal for verification.}

  \begin{block}{Cost Estimate}

    \begin{center}
      \begin{tabular}{lc}
        \toprule
         & \textbf{Cost (1 patient)} \\
        \midrule
        LLM compute (this run) & \$10--28 \\
        RA labor (abstraction) & $\sim$\$400 \\
        \midrule
        \textbf{Reduction}     & \textbf{$>$10$\times$} \\
        \bottomrule
      \end{tabular}
    \end{center}
    \vspace{0.1em}
    Compute excludes \textbf{turnaround-speed gains} (3 hr vs. 12 hr); token usage and cost were estimated from input size, with \textbf{$\sim$95\% of cost} coming from processing the clinical notes.
  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Limitations}

  \begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Single patient (n=1)}: one complex case, one clinic, one model;
        descriptive only, pending multi-patient, multi-clinician validation.
  \item \textbf{Token ceiling forced multi-shot prompting}: SecureGPT's
        128K-input / 4,096-output limit split large source documents across
        prompts, which may affect consistency versus single-context extraction.
  \item Cost is a \textbf{list-price estimate}; actual token cost is recoverable
        from SecureGPT logs.
\end{itemize}
\vspace{-0.6em}
  \end{block}

 \begin{block}{Conclusions}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item LLM extraction was \textbf{highly aligned} with human abstraction on the
      structural backbone: drug presence, episode timing, and status.
\item Disagreement localized to \textbf{discontinuation reason}, driven largely
      by input limitations rather than model error (only 1 of 17 a genuine LLM
      error).
\item Low-confidence flags concentrate errors, enabling a \textbf{triage
      workflow} in which RA's review only low-confidence extractions,
      focusing expert effort where the model is least certain.
\item A more than \textbf{tenfold cost reduction} supports feasibility for
      scaling with confidence-guided review.
\item Per-field source-reliability patterns motivate \textbf{multi-patient
      validation}, letting reconciliation weights adapt per patient rather than
      relying on a fixed source hierarchy.
\item These single-patient results establish \textbf{feasibility and an error
      profile}, not generalizable performance.
\item \textbf{Future directions:} machine-learning pre-extraction data
      cleaning; agentic and zero-shot extraction workflows; and dynamic
      source-reliability re-weighting to strengthen reconciliation.
  \end{itemize}
  \vspace{-0.6em}
\end{block}
