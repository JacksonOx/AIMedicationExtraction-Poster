  \begin{block}{Background}

    Converting unstructured longitudinal medication histories from the EHR into
    structured research data relies on \textbf{manual chart abstraction}, which
    is resource-intensive and constrains both retrospective and prospective
    studies. Increasingly capable large language models (LLMs) may reduce this
    burden, but their reliability against human abstraction is largely
    unquantified.

    \vspace{0.6em}
    We evaluated a \textbf{proof-of-concept LLM medication-extraction pipeline}
    against human chart abstraction in a pediatric immune behavioral health
    (IBH) setting, reporting agreement and adjudicated accuracy separately.

    \vspace{0.6em}
    \textbf{Two research questions (kept strictly separate):}
    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{Agreement:} How closely do the two methods produce the same output?
      \item \textbf{Accuracy:} Where they differ, who is correct?
    \end{itemize}

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Methods}

    \textbf{Design:} Single-patient pilot using a patient selected as one of
    the most complex medication-collection cases in the clinic. EHR data were
    pulled via STARR (Stanford's research extraction tool) from first visit
    (April 2024) through most recent encounter (June 2026).

    \vspace{0.8em}
    \textbf{AI Extraction and Adjudication Pipeline}

    \vspace{0.7em}
    \textbf{Extraction model:} Claude Opus 4.6 via SecureGPT (Stanford's
    PHI-compliant institutional gateway). Each source extracted independently;
    consolidated into one AI list using a confidence-weighted, source-aware
    reconciliation.

    \vspace{0.7em}
    \textbf{Comparator:} Independent human abstraction by IBH clinic research
    assistants (RAs), compared across three layers:
    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{Drug Presence}
      \item \textbf{Course Start and Stop Dates}
      \item \textbf{Discontinuation Reason}
    \end{itemize}

    \vspace{0.6em}
    \textbf{Adjudication:} Substantive disagreements judged by chart review as
    AI-correct, human-correct, both-defensible, or both-wrong, with cause
    classified.

    \vspace{0.6em}
    \textbf{Scope:} 21 medications analyzed. Medication classes the human
    workflow does not collect were excluded.

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Confidence vs.\ Validated Accuracy}

    Each contest was tagged with the AI episode's reconciliation confidence
    (high / medium / low). \textbf{Merit-loss rate} = (human beats it + both
    wrong) / total, excluding could-not-be-known ties.

    \vspace{0.5em}
    \begin{center}
      \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Confidence} & \textbf{N} & \textbf{AI correct} & \textbf{Human beats it} & \textbf{Merit-loss} \\
        \midrule
        High   & 6 & 2 & 0 & \textbf{0\%}  \\
        Medium & 5 & 2 & 1 & \textbf{20\%} \\
        Low    & 5 & 1 & 1 & \textbf{40\%} \\
        \bottomrule
      \end{tabular}
    \end{center}

    \vspace{0.4em}
    High confidence never lost on the merits (its 3 non-AI-correct outcomes
    were all could-not-be-known). The single genuine reasoning error sat at
    medium, indicating the model under-discriminates at the top. The
    \textbf{low-confidence tag is a reliable verify signal} for targeted human
    review.

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Conclusions}

    \begin{itemize}
      \item LLM extraction was \textbf{highly concordant} with human abstraction
            on the structural backbone: drug existence, episode timing, and
            medication status.
      \item Disagreement concentrated in \textbf{discontinuation reason (60\%)},
            largely attributable to input limitations rather than model error;
            only 1 of 17 disagreements was a genuine AI reasoning error.
      \item \textbf{Low-confidence flags concentrated genuine errors}, supporting
            a workflow in which humans review only low-confidence extractions.
      \item Estimated compute cost was \textbf{\$10--28 vs.\ $\sim$\$400 in RA
            labor}: a more than tenfold reduction.
      \item Findings are \textbf{descriptive (n=1)} and require multi-patient
            validation, but accuracy, cost, and time savings support feasibility
            for scaling with confidence-guided human review.
    \end{itemize}

  \end{block}
