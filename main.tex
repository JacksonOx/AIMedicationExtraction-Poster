  \begin{block}{Background}

    Reconstructing longitudinal medication histories from the electronic health
    record (EHR) for research relies on \textbf{labor-intensive manual chart
    abstraction}. Large language models (LLMs) may substantially reduce this
    burden, but their reliability against human abstraction is largely
    unquantified -- particularly in complex pediatric settings.

    \vspace{0.6em}
    \textbf{Setting:} The Stanford Children's Immune Behavioral Health (IBH) /
    PANS clinic manages patients with complex, multi-medication courses spanning
    psychiatric, DMARD, NSAID, steroid, and infusion therapies -- a demanding
    extraction target.

    \vspace{0.6em}
    \textbf{Two research questions (kept strictly separate):}
    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{Similarity:} How closely do the two methods agree?
      \item \textbf{Accuracy:} Where they differ, who is right?
    \end{itemize}

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Methods}

    \textbf{Design:} Single-patient pilot (patient 77-1120). Inter-method
    agreement study with manual adjudication. Review window: April 2024 through
    June 2026.

    \vspace{0.8em}
    \textbf{AI Extraction Pipeline}

    \vspace{0.5em}
    \begin{tikzpicture}[
      node distance=0.35cm and 0.9cm,
      src/.style={draw, rounded corners=4pt, fill=slatecol!15,
                  text width=3.5cm, align=center,

      proc/.style={draw, rounded corners=4pt, fill=aiteal!20,

    ]

      \node[proc, right=1.5cm of s2]  (o4) {Reconcile\\(O4)};
      \node[proc, right=1.5cm of o4]  (o5) {Compare\\(O5)};
      \node[human, above=0.6cm of o5] (hum){Human\\REDCap};

      \draw[arr] (s1.east) -- ++(0.5,0) |- (o4.west);
      \draw[arr] (s2.east) -- (o4.west);
      \draw[arr] (s3.east) -- ++(0.5,0) |- (o4.west);
      \draw[arr] (o4.east) -- (o5.west);
      \draw[arr] (hum.south) -- (o5.north);
    \end{tikzpicture}

    \vspace{0.7em}
    \textbf{Extraction model:} Claude Opus 4.6 via Stanford Secure GPT
    (PHI-compliant gateway / AWS Bedrock). Each source extracted independently;
    reconciled into one AI list (O4) using a confidence-weighted,
    source-aware model.

    \vspace{0.7em}
    \textbf{Three comparison layers:}
    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{Layer A -- Drug presence:} Was each medication captured
            by both methods?
      \item \textbf{Layer B -- Episode:} Did each start-to-stop course match?
      \item \textbf{Layer C -- Field-level:} Start date, stop date, dose,
            status, and discontinuation reason on matched pairs.
    \end{itemize}

    \vspace{0.6em}
    \textbf{Adjudication:} Each substantive disagreement (contest) was judged
    against the source chart as AI-correct, human-correct, both-defensible, or
    both-wrong, with cause classified into six root-cause categories.

    \vspace{0.6em}
    \textbf{Scope:} 21 medications analyzed. Miscellaneous / OTC items not
    collected by the human team and one after-window DMARD were excluded. All
    exclusions are documented.

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Similarity: Drug Presence (Layer A)}

    Of 21 AI-extracted medications, \textbf{19 (90.5\%)} were also captured by
    the human team. The AI missed \textbf{zero} medications the human team
    collected. Two AI-only drugs were confirmed real exposures:
    hydroxyzine (confirmed human omission) and lorazepam (documented dispensing
    only partially captured by both methods).

    \vspace{0.5em}
    Shared medications by class: 6 psychiatric, 4 NSAID, 4 DMARD, 3 steroid /
    infusion, 2 antibiotic.

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Similarity: Episode and Field Agreement (Layers B+C)}

    \heading{Episode matching (Layer B)}

    29 of 34 episodes (85.3\%) matched across methods. There were 4 AI-only
    episodes and 1 human-only pre-window trial. Among matched pairs, 8
    differences were recording-convention disagreements (dose-step granularity
    or dating style) rather than substantive errors.

    \vspace{0.7em}
    \heading{Date concordance (Layer C)}

    \begin{center}
      \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Field} & \textbf{Exact} & \textbf{$\leq$7 d} & \textbf{$\leq$30 d} & \textbf{Same Qtr} \\
        \midrule
        Start date & 69\% & 93.1\% & 96.6\% & 100\% \\
        Stop date  & 63\% & 89.5\% & 100\%  & 100\% \\
        \bottomrule
      \end{tabular}
    \end{center}

    \vspace{0.5em}
    Status agreed in 90\% of matched pairs (26/29). Discontinuation reason was
    the weakest field at 60\% (9/15); adjudication shows most disagreements
    reflect vocabulary differences or could-not-be-known information rather
    than model error.

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Source-by-Field Concordance (pre-adjudication)}

    \begin{center}

  \begin{block}{Confidence vs.\ Validated Accuracy}

    Each contest was tagged with the AI episode's reconciliation confidence
    (high / medium / low). \textbf{Merit-loss rate} = (human beats it + both
    wrong) / total, excluding could-not-be-known ties.

    \vspace{0.5em}
    \begin{center}
      \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Confidence} & \textbf{N} & \textbf{AI correct} & \textbf{Human beats it} & \textbf{Merit-loss} \\
        \midrule
        High   & 6 & 2 & 0 & \textbf{0\%}  \\
        Medium & 5 & 2 & 1 & \textbf{20\%} \\
        Low    & 5 & 1 & 1 & \textbf{40\%} \\
        \bottomrule
      \end{tabular}
    \end{center}

    \vspace{0.4em}
    High confidence never lost on the merits (its 3 non-AI-correct outcomes
    were all could-not-be-known). The single genuine reasoning error sat at
    medium, indicating the model under-discriminates at the top. The
    \textbf{low-confidence tag is a reliable verify signal} for targeted human
    review.

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Conclusions}

    \begin{itemize}
      \item The LLM pipeline achieved \textbf{high concordance with human
            abstraction} on drug presence (90.5\%), episode timing, and
            medication status.
      \item \textbf{The AI missed zero medications} captured by the human
            team; 2 AI-only drugs were real exposures the human team missed.
      \item Disagreement concentrated in \textbf{discontinuation reason
            (60\%)}, but adjudication shows most cases reflect vocabulary
            differences or input limitations -- not model error.
      \item \textbf{Low-confidence tags effectively identify} episodes
            warranting targeted human review (merit-loss 40\% vs.\ 0\% at
            high confidence).
      \item AI extraction is estimated to be \textbf{5--15$\times$ cheaper}
            than human abstraction before accounting for turnaround speed.
      \item Results are \textbf{descriptive (n=1)} and require multi-patient
            validation, but support feasibility for scaling with
            confidence-guided review.
    \end{itemize}

  \end{block}
