\begin{column}{\colwide}  

  \begin{block}{Background}

    Converting unstructured longitudinal medication histories from the EHR into
    structured research data relies on \textbf{manual chart abstraction}, a
    resource-intensive process that constrains retrospective and prospective
    studies alike and limits how readily these data can inform clinical
    decision-making. Increasingly capable large language models (LLMs) may
    reduce this burden, but their reliability against human abstraction remains
    largely unquantified.

    \vspace{0.4em}
    We evaluated a \textbf{proof-of-concept LLM medication-extraction pipeline}
    against human chart abstraction in pediatric immune behavioral health
    (IBH), a setting where patients carry long, frequently revised medication
    histories that make abstraction especially demanding. We report agreement
    and adjudicated accuracy separately.

    \vspace{0.4em}
    \textbf{Key research questions:}
    \vspace{-0.6em}
    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{Agreement / Alignment:} How often do the two methods
            produce the same result?
      \item \textbf{Accuracy:} Where they differ, which method is correct?
    \end{itemize}
    \vspace{-0.4em}
  \end{block}

  \vspace{-0.6em}
  \begin{block}{Methods}

    \textbf{Design:} \textbf{Single-patient pilot} in one of the clinic's most
    complex medication-collection cases. EHR and REDCap data spanning the
    patient's first visit (April 2024) through their most recent encounter
    (June 2026) were extracted via STARR (Stanford's research extraction tool)
    and manual CSV download, yielding \textbf{280 clinical notes} from a range
    of Stanford clinics, \textbf{39 patient-questionnaire responses}, and
    \textbf{313 Epic medication entries}. These \textbf{same source materials}
    were given to both the AI pipeline and the IBH clinic research assistants
    (RAs) who manually abstracted the medications, ensuring an equal-information
    comparison.

    \textbf{Extraction/Reconciliation:} Each source was extracted independently
    by Claude Opus 4.6 via SecureGPT (Stanford's PHI-compliant institutional
    gateway), then consolidated into a single AI list using a confidence-weighted,
    source-reliability-aware reconciliation.

    \vspace{0.6em}
    \textbf{Comparison:} Independent manual abstraction by the same RAs,
    compared against the AI list across three layers:

    \vspace{0.6em}
    \textbf{Adjudication:} Substantive disagreements were judged by chart review
    as AI-correct, human-correct, both-defensible, or both-wrong, with the cause
    of each classified.

    \vspace{0.6em}
    \textbf{Scope:} Across the sources and time frame the AI screened for
    \textbf{126 distinct medications} spanning the clinic's six tracked classes:
    DMARDs, antibiotics, psychiatric medications, IVIG, NSAIDs, and steroids.

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Agreement: Drug Presence}
    \begin{center}{\small
      \begin{tabular}{lc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Drug presence} & \textbf{Meds} \\
        \midrule
        Extracted by AI         & 21 \\
        Shared (AI + human)     & \textbf{19 (90.5\%)} \\
        Human-only (AI missed)  & \textbf{0} \\
        AI-only                 & \textbf{2} \\
        \bottomrule
      \end{tabular}}
    \end{center}
    \vspace{0.2em}
    Of \textbf{21} validated medications, \textbf{19 (90.5\%)} were recorded by
    both methods. The LLM \textbf{did not miss any} medication the RAs recorded
    and captured \textbf{two additional} validated medications.
  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Agreement: Dates}
    \begin{center}
\includesvg[width=0.95\colnarrow]{fig11_date_concordance}
    \end{center}
    \vspace{0.2em}
    Among matched episodes, dates converge quickly: \textbf{93\%} of start and
    \textbf{89\%} of stop dates agree within 7 days, and functionally every date
    fell within thirty days.
  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Agreement: Status and Sources}

    \textbf{Status.}
    Medication status (ongoing vs.\ stopped) agreed in \textbf{90\% (26/29)}.

    \vspace{0.5em}
    \textbf{Source reliability by field.}
    Concordance with the human varied by field, which the source-aware
    reconciliation exploits:

    \vspace{0.2em}
    \begin{center}{\small
      \begin{tabular*}{\linewidth}{@{\extracolsep{\fill}}lcc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Data Field} & \textbf{Best Source (agreement)} & \textbf{Avg. of 3 Sources} \\
        \midrule
        Start date & Epic Meds (\textbf{96\%})        & 84\% \\
        Stop date  & Clinical Notes (\textbf{81\%})   & 67\% \\
        Status     & Parent Reporting (\textbf{96\%}) & 92\% \\
        Reason     & Clinical Notes (\textbf{64\%})   & 48\% \\
        \bottomrule
      \end{tabular*}}
    \end{center}
    \vspace{0.2em}
    Each field has a clear best source in this patient, though weights likely
    vary across patients. Multi-patient data will allow these reliability
    patterns to inform a more dynamic, patient-aware reconciliation step.

  \end{block}

 \begin{block}{Accuracy: Adjudication (17 Contests)}
  \begin{center}
    \includesvg[width=0.98\colnarrow]{fig13_adjudication_donut}
  \end{center}
  \vspace{0.3em}
  \begin{center}
    \begin{tabular}{cc}
      \toprule
      \textbf{AI win rate} & \textbf{Genuine AI error} \\
      \midrule
      5/7 (71.4\%) & 1/17 (5.9\%) \\
      \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
  \end{center}
  \vspace{0.3em}
  \textbf{Root causes:} the AI was correct where the human missed or erred (5)
  or where the item lay outside human scope or timing (2); 5 stemmed from input
  or pipeline data unavailable in STARR; 3 were defensible clinical-judgment
  ties; 1 required clinician-only knowledge; only \textbf{1} was a genuine AI
  reasoning error.
\end{block}

  \begin{block}{Accuracy: Discontinuation Reason}

    Discontinuation reason was the weakest field, agreeing in \textbf{60\% (9/15)}.
    Adjudication shows most of that gap is \emph{not} model error:

    \vspace{0.3em}
    \begin{center}
\includesvg[width=0.95\colnarrow]{fig12_discontinuation_reason}
    \end{center}
    \vspace{0.2em}
    The disagreements are largely the same clinical event in different defensible
    vocabulary, or facts absent from the AI's inputs (such as an insurance-driven
    stop); only \textbf{one of 15} was a genuine AI mistake.

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Confidence Calibration}

    The AI's self-reported confidence tracks both its agreement with the human
    and its adjudicated accuracy:

    \vspace{0.3em}
    \begin{center}{\small
      \begin{tabular}{lcc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{AI confidence} & \textbf{Agreement} & \textbf{Merit-loss} \\
        \midrule
        High   & 92\%          & \textbf{0\%}  \\
        Medium & 89\%          & \textbf{20\%} \\
        Low    & \textbf{50\%} & \textbf{40\%} \\
        \bottomrule
      \end{tabular}}
    \end{center}
    \vspace{0.2em}
    {\small Agreement = concordance with the human; merit-loss = (human wins +
    both wrong) / contests at that level.}

    \vspace{0.3em}
    As confidence falls, agreement drops and merit-loss rises. High-confidence
    extractions never lost on the merits; the single genuine reasoning error sat
    at medium. \textbf{Low confidence is a reliable verify signal}: review the
    low-confidence extractions and trust the rest.

  \end{block}

\begin{column}{\colnarrow}   % COLUMN 4 (Discussion)

  \begin{block}{Cost Estimate}

    \begin{center}
      \begin{tabular}{lc}
        \toprule
         & \textbf{Cost (1 patient)} \\
        \midrule
        AI compute (this run)  & \$10--28 \\
        RA labor (abstraction) & $\sim$\$400 \\
        \midrule
        \textbf{Reduction}     & \textbf{$>$10$\times$} \\
        \bottomrule
      \end{tabular}
    \end{center}
    \vspace{0.3em}
    Compute excludes turnaround-speed gains; actual token usage is recoverable
    from SecureGPT session logs.

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Limitations}

    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{Single patient (n=1)}: one complex case, one clinic, one
            model; descriptive only and not yet generalizable.
      \item Requires \textbf{multi-patient, multi-clinician validation}.
      \item 3 of 17 contests were \textbf{unresolvable} from available inputs,
            bounding adjudication.
      \item Cost is a \textbf{list-price estimate}; actual token cost is
            recoverable from SecureGPT logs.
    \end{itemize}

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{Conclusions}

    \begin{itemize}
      \item LLM extraction was \textbf{highly concordant} with human abstraction
            on the structural backbone: drug presence, episode timing, and status.
      \item Disagreement localized to \textbf{discontinuation reason}, largely
            input limitations rather than model error (only 1 of 17 a genuine AI
            error).
      \item \textbf{Low-confidence flags concentrate errors}, enabling a workflow
            where humans review only low-confidence extractions.
      \item With a more than \textbf{tenfold cost reduction}, results support
            \textbf{feasibility for scaling} with confidence-guided review,
            pending validation.
    \end{itemize}

  \end{block}
